If your dog has been drinking gallons of water, asking to go out constantly, losing weight despite a good appetite, and your vet has now diagnosed diabetes, you're at the start of a real routine change — but also a very manageable one. Dogs do well with diabetes when their care is consistent, and consistency is something you can build.

## What canine diabetes is

Diabetes mellitus in dogs is, in the vast majority of cases, a deficiency of insulin — the hormone that lets cells take up sugar from the blood. Without enough of it, blood sugar climbs while the body's cells effectively starve, which is why a diabetic dog loses weight even while eating well and drinks and urinates excessively as the body tries to flush out excess sugar.

Unlike cats, who can sometimes go into diabetic remission, dogs with diabetes almost always need insulin injections for life. That sounds heavier than it is in practice — most owners find the twice-daily routine becomes second nature within a couple of weeks.

## What daily management looks like

Canine diabetes management rests on a few steady pillars:

- **Insulin injections**, typically twice daily, given under the skin after meals. The needles are tiny and most dogs barely notice.
- **Consistent feeding** — same food, same amounts, same times — so that insulin doses match predictable meals.
- **A stable routine** for exercise and daily life, since big swings in activity affect blood sugar.
- **Monitoring**, both at the vet and increasingly at home, to make sure the dose is right.

The watchword is consistency. Diabetes care isn't about perfection; it's about doing roughly the same thing at roughly the same times every day.

## What your vet monitors — and what you can track at home

Your vet will periodically assess how well the insulin dose is controlling blood sugar, sometimes with a blood glucose curve (a series of readings across a day) or a marker called fructosamine that reflects longer-term control. Many owners also learn to spot-check blood glucose at home, which reduces stress for the dog and gives the vet real-world numbers.

Between visits, the things worth tracking are the ones that reveal whether control is holding: insulin dose and time given, appetite, water intake, energy, and any home glucose readings. Excessive thirst creeping back, weight changes, or shifts in appetite are all signals that the dose may need revisiting.

You also need to know the signs of blood sugar dropping too low — weakness, wobbliness, disorientation, trembling, and in severe cases seizures. Low blood sugar is the one diabetic emergency that can't wait, so knowing those signs cold is part of the routine from day one.

## Why a log makes diabetes manageable

The dogs who stabilize fastest tend to belong to owners who kept simple, consistent records — not because more data is better, but because patterns are far easier to see on a chart than to reconstruct from memory.
