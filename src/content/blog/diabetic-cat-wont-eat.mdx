When a healthy cat skips a meal, you shrug it off. When a *diabetic* cat won't eat, it's a different situation entirely — because food and insulin are linked, and removing one side of that equation can tip your cat's blood sugar in a dangerous direction. Knowing why appetite matters so much, and what to do, takes the guesswork out of a stressful morning.

## Why a skipped meal is a bigger deal for a diabetic cat

Insulin lowers blood sugar. Food raises it. In a stable diabetic cat, the two are balanced — a dose of insulin is given on the assumption that the cat has eaten, or will. When a diabetic cat refuses food but still receives a normal insulin dose, blood sugar can drop too low, which is the hypoglycemia that every diabetic-cat owner rightly worries about.

That's why appetite isn't a side detail in feline diabetes — it's part of the safety system. A cat that suddenly won't eat is giving you important information before you draw up the next syringe.

## What to do when your cat won't eat

The first and most important step: **don't give insulin on autopilot.** If your cat hasn't eaten, the standard guidance is to contact your vet before giving the dose, because the right move depends on your cat's specific plan. Many vets advise giving a reduced dose or skipping it when a cat won't eat, but this is exactly the kind of decision that should come from your vet, not a guess — and never a rule you apply without checking.

Then, look at the bigger picture. Is this a one-off, or has appetite been sliding for a few days? Is your cat otherwise bright, or also lethargic, vomiting, or hiding? A single refused meal in an otherwise normal cat is different from a cat who's been eating less and less, and that context shapes how urgent the situation is.

## When loss of appetite is an emergency

Some situations mean calling your vet promptly rather than waiting:

- Your cat hasn't eaten for **more than 24 hours**
- Refusal to eat alongside **vomiting, lethargy, or weakness**
- Any signs of low blood sugar — wobbliness, disorientation, trembling
- A cat that won't eat and seems generally unwell

Cats are also prone to a serious liver complication (hepatic lipidosis) when they go without food, so prolonged refusal to eat is never something to simply wait out in any cat, diabetic or not.

Appetite is often the earliest signal that something's shifting in a diabetic cat — frequently showing up before a glucose reading would catch it.
