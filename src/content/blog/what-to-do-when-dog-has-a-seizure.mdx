Few things are as frightening as watching your dog have a seizure for the first time — the collapse, the paddling, the loss of awareness. It feels like forever even when it lasts under a minute. If your dog has epilepsy or has just had a first seizure, knowing exactly what to do ahead of time is what lets you stay useful instead of frozen.

## In the moment: what actually helps

The hardest instinct to fight is the urge to grab and hold your dog. During a seizure, the most helpful things are calm and simple.

**Keep your hands away from the mouth.** Dogs cannot swallow their tongues, and a seizing dog may bite without any awareness of doing it. **Clear the area** of anything hard or sharp, and if your dog is near stairs or a ledge, gently guide them away from the danger — without restraining their movements. **Dim the lights and lower the noise** if you can. **Note the time** the seizure starts; this matters more than it feels like it should.

Most seizures end on their own within a couple of minutes. Afterward, your dog may be disoriented, wobbly, temporarily blind, or ravenously hungry for minutes to hours — this recovery phase is normal and not painful, even though it looks unsettling. Speak softly and let them come back to themselves.

## When a seizure is an emergency

Most single seizures, while alarming, aren't immediately life-threatening. But certain situations are true emergencies and mean getting to a vet right away:

- A seizure lasting **longer than 5 minutes** (status epilepticus)
- **Multiple seizures** in a row without full recovery between them (cluster seizures)
- More than a usual number of seizures in a 24-hour period
- A first-ever seizure, which always warrants a vet visit to find the cause

This is the reason noting the start time matters so much: in the moment, two minutes feels like ten, and the actual duration is exactly what determines whether you're watching something routine or something urgent.

## Why your vet needs a seizure log

Epilepsy is managed largely on patterns, and your vet wasn't in the room. When they're deciding whether to start medication, change a dose, or investigate further, the questions are specific: How often? How long? At what time of day? Any trigger beforehand? How long was the recovery? Clustered or single?

Almost no one can answer those reliably from memory across months — especially in the adrenaline-soaked aftermath of a seizure, when details blur. A log of dates, times, durations, and circumstances is genuinely one of the most clinically valuable things you can hand a vet treating a seizure disorder. It's how dose adjustments get made on evidence instead of guesswork.
