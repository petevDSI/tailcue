If your older cat has been losing weight while eating more than ever — maybe with a new restlessness, a scruffier coat, or more vocalizing at night — and your vet has now used the word "hyperthyroidism," you're dealing with the most common hormonal disease in cats. It's also one of the most treatable, which is the part worth holding onto as you take in everything else.

## What's actually happening

Hyperthyroidism means the thyroid glands in your cat's neck are producing too much thyroid hormone (thyroxine, or T4). According to AAHA and AAFP guidance, this is almost always caused by benign overgrowth of the thyroid tissue — not cancer, which accounts for fewer than 3% of cases. The excess hormone revs the entire metabolism up, which is why these cats famously eat voraciously yet keep losing weight, and often develop a fast heart rate and high blood pressure over time.

It's typically diagnosed in cats older than 10, and because the signs overlap with other senior-cat conditions like kidney disease, your vet leans on a blood test — total T4 — to confirm it.

## How it's diagnosed and why one test isn't always enough

Diagnosis rests on a persistently elevated total T4 measurement combined with clinical signs. Most hyperthyroid cats have T4 levels well above the normal range, which makes the diagnosis straightforward. But in some cats the first reading sits only at the high end of normal — sometimes because a concurrent illness is masking the number — so AAHA guidance recommends rechecking T4 in one to two weeks, and occasionally adding a free T4 test, before settling the diagnosis. If your vet wants to repeat bloodwork before committing to treatment, that caution is by design.

## The treatment paths

There are four main options, and your vet will help you weigh them against your cat's age, other health issues, and your circumstances:

- **Daily medication** (methimazole, oral or transdermal) controls hormone levels but doesn't cure the disease; it's lifelong and requires periodic monitoring.
- **Radioactive iodine (I-131)** is considered the gold-standard cure for most cats — a single treatment that destroys the overactive tissue, usually with one short hospital stay.
- **Prescription diet** (severely iodine-restricted) can manage the disease in some cats who eat nothing else.
- **Surgery** to remove the thyroid tissue is less common now but still used in specific cases.

None of these is automatically "right" — it's a genuine conversation to have with your vet.

## What gets monitored, and what you can watch at home

Whichever path you choose, your vet will recheck T4 periodically to make sure levels land in the target range — not too high, and not pushed so low that your cat tips into the opposite problem. They'll also keep an eye on kidney values, because treating hyperthyroidism can unmask underlying kidney disease that the overactive metabolism was hiding.

At home, you're the one who sees the day-to-day. The signs that tell you treatment is working are the reverse of the ones that brought you in: weight stabilizing or recovering, appetite settling toward normal, less restlessness, a calmer heart, a better coat. Tracking weight, appetite, and energy week to week gives your vet a real picture at each recheck instead of a vague impression.
