You glance over at your sleeping dog and notice their belly is moving quickly — faster than it used to. Maybe you've already started counting. If your dog has a heart murmur or a heart condition, this is exactly the thing worth paying attention to, because a rising sleeping breathing rate is one of the earliest and most reliable warning signs that fluid is building up in the lungs.

## The one number to know

For dogs with heart disease, veterinary cardiologists rely heavily on the **sleeping respiratory rate (SRR)** — the number of breaths per minute while your dog is genuinely asleep. The consensus across veterinary cardiology is clear: a healthy dog, or a dog whose heart failure is well controlled with medication, has a sleeping or resting breathing rate consistently **under 30 breaths per minute**. Many normal dogs sit comfortably in the 15–25 range.

A sleeping rate that is consistently **over 30 breaths per minute** is considered abnormal and, in a dog with known heart disease, is often the first sign that congestive heart failure is starting or returning. Research presented to the American College of Veterinary Internal Medicine found that dogs with stable, medically controlled left-sided heart failure reliably keep their sleeping rates below 30 — and a climb above that threshold is what prompts a same-day call.

## How to count it the right way

The number is only useful if you measure it correctly, and the most common mistakes make a normal dog look alarming.

Count only when your dog is **sleeping or lying calmly at rest** — never right after a walk, never while panting, never in a hot room. Panting is not the same as breathing rate and will give you a falsely high number. Watch the chest or belly rise and fall; one full in-and-out cycle counts as a single breath. Count for a full 30 seconds and multiply by two, or count a full 60 seconds for the most accurate figure.

Doing this a few times a week, when your dog is relaxed, gives you a personal baseline. That baseline is what makes a real change obvious.

## When the number means "call today"

If your dog's sleeping breathing rate is consistently above 30 breaths per minute across more than one count — especially paired with coughing, reluctance to lie down, restlessness at night, or a belly that looks more distended — that's a same-day call to your veterinarian. A single high count when your dog was dreaming or warm isn't the same thing; it's the consistent trend that matters.

Open-mouth breathing at rest, gums that look blue or gray, or genuine struggle to breathe are emergencies — go straight to an emergency clinic.
