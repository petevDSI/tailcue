If your older dog has been drinking and peeing far more than usual, developing a pot-bellied look, losing hair symmetrically along the flanks, and acting hungrier than ever, your vet may have diagnosed Cushing's disease. It's one of the more common hormonal disorders in middle-aged and senior dogs, and because its signs creep in slowly, plenty of owners write them off as "just getting old" until they add up.

## What Cushing's actually is

Cushing's disease — hyperadrenocorticism — means the body is producing too much cortisol, the stress hormone. Most often this is driven by a small benign tumor on the pituitary gland that keeps signaling the adrenal glands to overproduce; less commonly it comes from a tumor on an adrenal gland itself. A third form is iatrogenic, caused by long-term steroid medication.

Chronic excess cortisol is what produces the classic picture: increased thirst and urination, a ravenous appetite, a swollen or pot-bellied abdomen, thinning skin and symmetrical hair loss, panting, and muscle weakness. Individually each sign is easy to dismiss; together they form a recognizable pattern.

## Why diagnosis takes more than one test

Cushing's is notoriously tricky to confirm, and that's not your vet being indecisive. Screening usually starts with bloodwork and urine tests, then moves to specific hormone-stimulation tests (such as an ACTH stimulation test or a low-dose dexamethasone suppression test) — sometimes more than one, because no single test is perfect and other conditions can mimic the results. Imaging may follow to look for the source. If the workup feels drawn out, it's because confirming Cushing's correctly matters before committing a dog to lifelong medication.

## Treatment and the balancing act it requires

The most common treatment for pituitary-dependent Cushing's is a daily medication (trilostane) that reduces cortisol production. Adrenal-dependent cases are sometimes treated surgically. The goal isn't to eliminate cortisol — dogs need some — but to bring it back into a normal range.

That balance is why monitoring is so central to Cushing's. Too little control and the symptoms persist; too much suppression and a dog can swing toward the opposite, potentially dangerous problem of too little cortisol. Your vet will schedule recheck bloodwork (often ACTH stimulation tests) to fine-tune the dose, sometimes several times in the first months as they dial it in.

## What you watch for at home

Because the medication dose is a moving target, your day-to-day observations genuinely guide treatment. The signs that tell you it's working are the reverse of the ones you started with: thirst and urination returning toward normal, appetite settling, energy improving, the pot-belly receding, coat regrowing over time.

Just as important are the warning signs of *over*-treatment, which your vet will brief you on: lethargy, loss of appetite, vomiting, or diarrhea can mean cortisol has dropped too low and warrant a prompt call. Tracking water intake, appetite, and energy gives your vet the trend they need to adjust the dose safely.
