A new diabetes diagnosis is a lot to take in. Between learning to give injections, adjusting to a feeding schedule, and watching for warning signs, it's easy to lose track of what actually happened on any given day — and that information matters more than it might seem.

Most vets will ask for some version of a glucose curve or a log of readings, insulin doses, and appetite over the days or weeks before a recheck. The cats who tend to stabilize fastest are usually the ones whose owners kept consistent, simple records — not because more data is automatically better, but because patterns are much easier to see on paper than to remember.

## What's actually worth tracking

You don't need to log everything. Three numbers matter most:

**Blood glucose**, if you're home-testing. Even a few readings a week, taken at consistent times, tell your vet far more than one isolated number from a stressful clinic visit.

**Insulin dose**, exactly as given — units and time. Doses sometimes get adjusted mid-treatment, and a clear record prevents confusion about what was actually administered versus what was prescribed.

**Appetite**, in simple terms: poor, normal, or ravenous. Appetite changes are often the earliest sign that something's shifting, frequently showing up before a glucose reading would catch it.

## Knowing when a number is actually a problem

This is where a lot of owners feel stuck — a reading looks low, but is it *call-the-vet* low, or just a number to note and watch? General veterinary guidance for diabetic cats treats blood glucose under 80 mg/dL as outside the target range and worth watching closely, with anything under 60 mg/dL considered an urgent signal to contact your vet. On the high end, readings consistently above 250–300 mg/dL also warrant a conversation, even though they're less immediately dangerous than a true low.

Your vet may set different specific targets for your cat — always defer to their individual plan over any general rule of thumb.
