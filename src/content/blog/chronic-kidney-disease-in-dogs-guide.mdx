When a vet says your dog has chronic kidney disease, the word "chronic" is doing a lot of quiet work. It means this is a long-term condition to manage, not necessarily a short timeline — and many dogs live comfortably for months or years with the right monitoring. Understanding what the diagnosis means takes a lot of the fear out of it.

## What's happening and how it's staged

The kidneys filter waste from the blood and manage hydration. In chronic kidney disease (CKD), that filtering capacity declines gradually and permanently. The goal of treatment isn't to reverse it — that's not possible — but to slow the progression and keep your dog feeling well at each stage.

Veterinarians worldwide use the **IRIS staging system** (International Renal Interest Society) to describe how advanced the disease is. Staging is based mainly on a blood marker called creatinine, increasingly alongside a more sensitive early marker called SDMA, and then sub-staged by two other findings: protein in the urine and blood pressure. In broad terms:

- **Stage 1** is the earliest, often before obvious symptoms, usually caught on screening bloodwork.
- **Stage 2** involves mild kidney value elevation and subtle signs.
- **Stage 3** brings more noticeable symptoms as waste accumulates.
- **Stage 4** is advanced disease requiring the most intensive management.

Knowing your dog's IRIS stage matters because it shapes everything — diet, medications, monitoring frequency, and what to expect.

## What your vet monitors

CKD management is built on periodic rechecks. Your vet will track kidney values (creatinine and SDMA), urine concentration and protein, blood pressure, electrolytes like phosphorus and potassium, and red blood cell counts, since kidneys play a role in producing them. The frequency depends on the stage — early disease might mean checks every few months, advanced disease much more often.

Treatment commonly includes a therapeutic kidney diet (lower in phosphorus and modified protein), medications to manage blood pressure or urinary protein, and steps to support hydration. Each piece is adjusted based on those rechecks.

## What you watch for at home

Between visits, you're the one who notices how your dog is actually doing. The signs worth tracking are practical and observable: water intake and urination (CKD typically increases both), appetite, weight, energy, and any vomiting or nausea. A drop in appetite or new vomiting can signal that waste products are building up and the plan may need adjusting — and it's the kind of change your vet wants to hear about sooner rather than at the next scheduled visit.

Hydration is central in CKD, so changes in how much your dog drinks are genuinely informative, not just trivia. So is weight, which tends to slip gradually in ways that are hard to notice day to day but obvious on a chart.
