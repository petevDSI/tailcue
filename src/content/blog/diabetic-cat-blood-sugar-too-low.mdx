If you're caring for a diabetic cat, the fear of giving too much insulin probably sits in the back of your mind every time you draw up a dose. It's a reasonable fear — low blood sugar, or hypoglycemia, is the one diabetic complication that can become an emergency in minutes rather than days. The good news is that it's recognizable, and knowing what to watch for takes most of the panic out of it.

## What hypoglycemia looks like

A cat with dangerously low blood sugar doesn't usually collapse without warning. The signs tend to build, and catching the early ones is what keeps a scary moment from becoming a crisis.

Early signs are subtle: unusual quietness, wobbliness, or a cat that seems "off" or disoriented. As blood sugar drops further, you may see weakness, trembling, a glazed or vacant expression, and unsteadiness on the feet. The most serious signs — and the ones that mean you act immediately — are seizures, collapse, or unresponsiveness.

These signs often appear a few hours after an insulin injection, when the dose is having its strongest effect, but they can happen any time the balance between insulin, food, and activity tips too far.

## What the numbers actually mean

If you home-test, this is where a glucometer earns its keep. General veterinary guidance for diabetic cats treats a blood glucose reading under 80 mg/dL as below the target range and worth watching closely, and a reading under 60 mg/dL as an urgent signal to act and contact your vet right away. A cat showing any of the serious signs above should be treated as an emergency regardless of what a meter says — never wait for a reading to confirm a seizure.

Your vet may set a different specific threshold for your individual cat. Their plan always comes first; these are general rules of thumb for when you're unsure.

## What to do in the moment

If your cat is alert enough to swallow safely, the standard first step is to rub a sugar source — corn syrup or honey — onto the gums, then offer food. If your cat is having a seizure or is unconscious, do not put anything in their mouth; the standard guidance is to rub a small amount of syrup on the gums only if it can be done safely, and get to an emergency vet immediately.

In every case, this is a call-your-vet moment. Hypoglycemia can rebound, and a cat that perks up after sugar still needs professional eyes on them. Your vet will also want to know it happened, because it often means the insulin dose needs revisiting.
