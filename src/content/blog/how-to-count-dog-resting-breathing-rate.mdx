If your dog has a heart murmur or heart disease, your vet has probably asked you to monitor their "resting respiratory rate" or "sleeping breathing rate" at home. It sounds clinical, but it's genuinely one of the simplest and most valuable things you can do — a free, at-home measurement that often catches trouble days before a dog looks sick. The catch is that it only works if you count it correctly.

## Why this number is so powerful

When the left side of the heart starts failing, fluid backs up into the lungs. Before a dog shows obvious distress, that early fluid makes them breathe a little faster while at rest. Veterinary cardiologists rely on this because it's an early-warning signal you can read at home — well before coughing or labored breathing appear.

The consensus is consistent across veterinary cardiology: a healthy dog, or one whose heart failure is well managed, keeps a sleeping or resting breathing rate **under 30 breaths per minute**, with many normal dogs sitting around 15 to 25. A rate consistently **over 30** in a dog with heart disease is a recognized signal to call the vet.

## How to count it correctly

The number is only meaningful when measured the right way, and a few simple rules prevent false alarms.

**1. Wait until your dog is sleeping or lying completely calm.** Sleeping is best — very little affects breathing rate during sleep. Never count right after exercise, eating, or excitement.

**2. Make sure they're not panting.** Panting is not breathing rate, and counting a panting dog will give you a uselessly high number. Choose a cool, quiet moment.

**3. Watch the chest or belly rise and fall.** One full cycle — in and out — counts as a single breath. Don't count the in and the out separately.

**4. Count for 30 seconds and multiply by two**, or count a full 60 seconds for the most accuracy.

**5. Do it a few times a week** when your dog is relaxed. This builds a personal baseline, which is what makes a real change obvious later.

## What the number tells you

Once you know your dog's normal resting range, deviations mean something. A reading or two above 30 — especially across more than one count, and especially with coughing, restlessness at night, or reluctance to lie down — is a same-day call to your vet. A single high count when your dog was warm or dreaming isn't the same thing; the trend matters more than any one number.

Open-mouth breathing at rest, blue or gray gums, or visible struggle to breathe are emergencies — go straight to an emergency clinic.
