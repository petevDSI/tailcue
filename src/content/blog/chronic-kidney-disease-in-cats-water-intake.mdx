Chronic kidney disease is one of the most common conditions affecting older cats, and a diagnosis often lands hard — partly because the word "chronic" sounds so final. It isn't. Many cats live comfortably with kidney disease for years when it's caught and managed well, and a few simple things you track at home make a real difference in how that management goes.

## What's happening in your cat's body

The kidneys filter waste from the blood and control hydration. In chronic kidney disease (CKD), that filtering ability declines slowly and permanently. Because cats are masters at hiding illness, CKD is often well underway before obvious signs appear — which is exactly why senior-cat bloodwork screening matters so much.

The earliest visible signs are usually increased thirst and urination. As the kidneys lose the ability to concentrate urine, cats drink more to keep up, and you may notice bigger clumps in the litter box and a water bowl that empties faster. Weight loss, reduced appetite, and lethargy tend to follow as the disease progresses.

## How it's staged and monitored

Vets worldwide use the **IRIS staging system** (International Renal Interest Society) to describe how advanced CKD is, based primarily on a blood marker called creatinine — increasingly alongside a more sensitive early marker, SDMA — and then refined by urine protein and blood pressure. The stage shapes the whole plan: diet, medications, and how often your cat is rechecked.

Treatment commonly centers on a therapeutic kidney diet (lower phosphorus, modified protein), supporting hydration, and medications to manage blood pressure or other complications. Your vet will recheck kidney values, urine, blood pressure, and electrolytes periodically, adjusting as the picture changes.

## Why water intake is the metric to watch

Of all the things you can track at home, water intake is one of the most telling in feline CKD. Because the disease drives increased thirst, changes in how much your cat drinks reflect what's happening inside — and shifts in appetite and weight fill in the rest of the picture.

This is also where memory fails badly. "She seems to be drinking more lately" is an impression, and impressions are unreliable for something that changes gradually. A cat slowly drinking more over a month, or slowly eating less, is giving you real information — but only if you can actually see the trend rather than guess at it. Appetite and weight are equally easy to misjudge day to day and equally important; weight in particular tends to slip in ways that are obvious on a chart and invisible in the moment.
